Faculty-led capstone engagement in health technology innovation, in which student teams work with early-stage health tech companies to identify strategic problems, conduct market and competitive analysis, and develop actionable commercialization recommendations. Letter grading.

Expanded Description
This is a two-quarter capstone where you do real strategic work for real early-stage health tech companies. No simulations. No hypothetical case studies. Five teams of five students, each embedded with a company working on problems that matter: helping premature infants develop language through acoustic monitoring in the NICU, bringing rapid point-of-care sickle cell diagnostics to underserved communities, developing antimicrobial smart textiles that could change how hospitals prevent infections, building robotic companions for people living with dementia, and creating novel blood collection devices for clinical laboratories. Your team works directly with company leadership to scope the problem, conduct primary and secondary research, build the analysis, and deliver recommendations the company can actually implement. This is the kind of work that shapes product launches, funding decisions, and market entry strategies.

What makes this course different from a typical classroom strategy project is threefold. First, it's AI-native. You won't just be permitted to use AI tools; you'll be expected to build with them. You'll set up persistent AI research environments, build custom analytical tools, design multi-step workflows, and learn where AI accelerates your work and where it produces confident-sounding nonsense that requires your judgment to catch. It's how high-performing professionals will be expected to work in a post-AI business environment. By the end, you'll have a portfolio of AI-augmented strategy tools and the fluency to build more. Second, it's grounded in healthcare's unique dynamics. Health tech doesn't always commercialize like consumer tech or enterprise SaaS. You'll learn enough about regulation, reimbursement, multi-stakeholder purchasing, and value demonstration to do credible work in the space, even if you've never worked in healthcare before. Third, it's market-driven. Every analysis starts from the market back, not the technology forward. The question is never "what can this product do?" It's "what does the market need, and how does this product fit?"

The first quarter is the foundations quarter: structured weekly sessions pairing common curriculum with team project work, building the analytical toolkit and healthcare fluency you need to do the job well. The second quarter is the delivery quarter: teams go deeper into company work, refine their recommendations through peer critique rounds, present drafts to company leadership for feedback, and deliver a final strategic presentation and written deliverable. If you're looking for a capstone that builds real skills, produces a real deliverable, and gives you an authentic operator experience at the intersection of healthcare, technology, and strategy, this is it.

Course Objectives
This capstone course is intended to help students apply the frameworks and tools acquired in the program to real-life business issues. At the end of the course, students will be able to:

1. Conduct rigorous market analysis for early-stage health tech companies, including TAM/SAM/SOM sizing with healthcare-specific funnels, customer segmentation, and competitive positioning grounded in primary and secondary research.
2. Develop actionable strategies for go-to-market and business model design that account for the unique dynamics of healthcare commercialization, including regulatory pathways, reimbursement economics, multi-stakeholder purchasing, and institutional buying cycles.
3. Use AI tools as core instruments of strategic analysis and communication, including building persistent AI research environments, designing custom analytical workflows, and exercising judgment about when AI output is reliable and when it requires human validation.
4. Build working healthcare fluency in the regulatory, reimbursement, and market dynamics that shape health tech commercialization.
5. Deliver executive-quality recommendations that lead with the answer, structure supporting arguments logically, and tailor the message to the specific constraints and opportunities of an early-stage company.
6. Function effectively on a consulting team, managing client relationships, giving and receiving substantive strategic feedback, resolving team dynamics issues, and maintaining professional standards throughout a multi-quarter engagement.
7. Synthesize analytical frameworks from across the MBA curriculum (finance, marketing, operations, strategy) and apply them to complex, ambiguous, real-world problems where the "right answer" is not predetermined.
8. Articulate personal development goals, assess progress against them with intellectual honesty, and reflect critically on how the experience shaped their thinking as a leader.

AI Usage Policy
This course treats AI as a core professional tool. You are expected to use AI throughout your project work: for research, analysis, tool-building, drafting, and iteration. The question is not whether you use AI, but how well you use it and how clearly you exercise judgment about its outputs.

What's expected:
- Set up and maintain a Claude Project as your team's persistent analytical environment. Load it with company documents, research findings, and well-crafted system prompts.
- Build custom AI tools and workflows for your specific analytical needs: market sizing models, interview guide generators, competitive intelligence agents, scenario analysis tools.
- Use AI to draft, structure, and iterate on deliverables. Use it to structure your strategic narrative so it leads with the recommendation and builds a logical argument.
- Document your AI methods. Every major team deliverable includes an AI methods appendix: what tools you built, how you used them, where AI was most and least valuable, and where your judgment overrode an AI recommendation.

What's non-negotiable:
- Every factual claim in a deliverable must be verified against a primary source. AI is unreliable at producing specific numbers, statistics, and citations. "Claude said so" is not a citation.
- Strategic judgment is yours. The recommendation must reflect your team's analysis and reasoning. If you cannot defend a recommendation without referring to what the AI suggested, you have not done the work.
- Peer feedback must be written by humans. Do not use AI to generate your peer critique forms.
- Your individual reflection must be authentically yours.

How to cite AI usage:
For all assignments, include a brief AI acknowledgment noting which tools you used and how. For major deliverables, include the AI methods appendix. You do not need to cite every individual prompt, but your documentation should give a reader a clear picture of your AI workflow.

The line between AI-augmented work and AI-replacing-the-thinking:
AI-augmented work: you form a hypothesis, use AI to research it, discover the hypothesis is wrong, revise your thinking, use AI to structure the revised argument, verify the evidence, and deliver a recommendation you can defend. AI-replacing-the-thinking: you ask AI to "develop a go-to-market strategy for this company," paste the output into your deliverable, and present it as your analysis. That's not strategic consulting. That's copy-paste with extra steps, and it will be obvious in the quality of the work.

If you have questions about what is and isn't appropriate, ask.

Students are individually evaluated based on performance on graded assignments and participation in course activities. Individual team members can and do receive different grades. Students will receive an in-process grade for the first term of this course, and grades will be assigned at the close of the second term.

Your overall course grade will be determined by how your performance on graded assignments ranks in comparison with other students in the class according to the grade distribution model at Anderson. Note that courses in which an overall grade of C is received must be offset by higher grades in the same term for students to remain in good academic standing at UCLA.

Individual grades on team deliverables may be adjusted based on peer evaluations to reflect relative contribution. The mid-course 360 peer evaluation (end of Q1) is purely developmental. The final 360 peer evaluation (end of Q2) can adjust an individual's team deliverable grade up or down by up to one-third of a letter grade.

Grading Criteria for Consulting Project Deliverables
A-range work is distinguished by: rigorous, multi-sourced analysis with verified data and stress-tested assumptions; specific, sequenced, and immediately actionable recommendations; deep grounding in market reality with evidence of stakeholder conversations; realistic feasibility assessment; clear structured communication that leads with the answer; and transparent, sophisticated AI tool usage that demonstrates genuine judgment.

B-range work covers the major issues competently with adequate data sources and reasonable recommendations, but may lack the depth, specificity, or stakeholder grounding of A-range work.

C-range work relies on thin analysis, single source types, or unverified claims. Recommendations are vague, generic, or disconnected from the evidence.

Assignment Descriptions

Personal Goal Statement
A 1-2 page document articulating what you want to get out of the course: specific skills to develop, areas of growth, how this connects to your career trajectory, and what you plan to contribute to your team. Goals that are specific, measurable, and connected to both your career trajectory and your team's project exceed the standard. Due by Week 2, submitted via Course Site.

Class Participation
Your attendance, quality of engagement with readings and cases, contributions to class discussion, peer critique quality (Q2), AI toolkit demo contributions, and professionalism in company interactions. The instructor maintains a running participation log across four dimensions: class discussion quality, peer critique quality, AI demo contribution, and company professionalism. Assessed throughout both quarters.

Osteoboost Case Analysis
An individual 2-3 page written analysis of the Osteoboost HBS case, which covers go-to-market strategy for an early-stage medical device startup choosing among five distinct GTM paths under capital constraints. Apply course frameworks to take a clear position on which GTM path the company should pursue and defend it with evidence. Connect the Osteoboost situation to your own company's challenges where relevant. AI tools may be used for research and structuring, but the analysis must reflect your own judgment. Due by Week 7, submitted via Course Site.

Project Scope and Workplan
A team deliverable: 3-5 page document defining the strategic question, deliverables, research plan, week-by-week workplan, and division of labor. Company sponsor acknowledgment required. Due by Week 3, submitted via Course Site.

Strategic Direction Presentation (Q1)
A 20-25 minute team presentation in Week 8. Each team presents their strategic direction, key findings, emerging recommendations, open questions, and Q2 work plan. Evaluated on whether the direction is defensible, grounded in evidence, and honest about uncertainties. Deck due by start of class via Course Site.

Strategic Direction Document (Q1)
A team deliverable: 5-10 page written articulation of strategic recommendations, supported by quarter-long research. Includes AI methods appendix, Q2 work plan, and team reflection. Due one week after Week 10, submitted via Course Site.

360 Peer Evaluations
Two rounds across four dimensions: intellectual contribution, work ethic, collaboration, and AI tool development. Mid-course evaluation (end of Week 10) is developmental. Final evaluation (end of Week 20) can adjust individual team grades by up to one-third of a letter grade.

Draft Strategic Recommendations (Q2)
A team progress checkpoint at approximately 60-70% completion. Evaluated on progress and trajectory. Due by Week 16, submitted via Course Site.

Final Presentation to Company (Q2)
A 30-minute team presentation (plus 15-minute Q&A) to company leadership and a faculty panel in Week 20. Evaluated by instructor (60%), company (25%), and panel (15%).

Final Written Strategic Deliverable (Q2)
A 15-30 page strategic document (plus appendices) the company can use. Includes updated AI methods appendix and an organized compendium of all research, analysis, tools, and supporting materials for the company to use after the engagement. Evaluated by instructor (70%) and company (30%). Due by end of Week 20, submitted via Course Site.

Individual Final Reflection
A 3-4 page reflective essay mapping back to your Week 1 goal statement. Addresses learning, skills development, honest self-assessment, and how your understanding of AI evolved. Due by end of Week 20, submitted individually via Course Site. Not shared with teammates.

Client Management Assessment
Team's professional relationship with company sponsor, evaluated throughout both quarters by instructor observation (60%) and company feedback (40%).
